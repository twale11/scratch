1. Executive Summary

The Sip & Scratch promotion was a clear success as a retention and engagement tool. Of 1,353 respondents, 82.5% received and redeemed their card — an exceptional activation rate for a physical in-box promotion. Nearly half of all respondents (47.5%) found the gamification concept more appealing than typical promotions, and only 2.3% actively disliked it.

The most telling statistic: 39.3% of respondents were at-risk (considering pausing, canceling, or uncertain about January). Of those 532 at-risk members, 267 (50.2%) said the promotion had moderate or major influence on their decision to stay — including 93 who explicitly said it gave them a reason to stay when they were considering pausing/skipping. This is a strong signal that the promotion meaningfully moved the needle on January retention.

That said, the survey also surfaced recurring pain points around QR code failures, the perceived low value of 100 base points, and a desire for more tangible rewards beyond points. These offer clear avenues to amplify the impact of future iterations.

2. Retention Influence — The Core Question

When we isolate the members who were genuinely at risk of churning, the promotion's value becomes much clearer:

Key Retention Finding

Of the 532 at-risk members (those who were uncertain, thinking about pausing, or actively considering canceling):

— 93 (17.5%) said the scratch card was a major influence that gave them a reason to stay

— 174 (32.7%) said it had a moderate influence, reinforcing their decision

— Combined, 50.2% of at-risk members were positively influenced to retain their subscription

3. Receipt, Redemption & Appeal

The 82.5% redemption rate is strong. However, 136 members (10%) received a card but didn't redeem it, and 100 members (7.4%) either didn't receive one or weren't sure. Both groups represent addressable gaps.

Top operational issues: The two largest barriers to redemption were not noticing the card in the box (59) and QR code / redemption process failures (57). These are both fixable problems that could recover an additional ~8% of participants in future runs.

Concept Appeal

The gamification angle resonated well: 47.5% found it more appealing than typical promotions, with only 16.6% preferring straightforward discounts. The scratch card format tapped into a sense of surprise and play that many members appreciated.

4. Improvement Preferences

The top request by a significant margin was more guaranteed minimum points (572 selections) — reflecting widespread disappointment with the 100-point base prize. This was followed by higher maximum prizes (434) and instant discounts instead of points (335). Only 240 members said no change was needed, and 206 would prefer a digital format.

5. Key Pain Points & Problems

🔴 QR Code / Redemption Failures

57+ members reported the QR code or redemption process didn't work. Many described invalid codes, being redirected to login pages, or codes showing as "already redeemed." Several gave up entirely.

"My code said it was invalid no matter how I input it (0 vs O etc)"
"It sent me to my login page, then to the home page. There was nothing to redeem it."

🔴 Card Visibility in Box

59+ members didn't notice the card. Cards were accidentally discarded with packaging, hidden under bottles, or mistaken for inserts. Some partners/family members threw them away.

"My husband threw out the card accidentally"
"I deconstructed the box. There was no scratch card in there."

🟡 100-Point Floor Too Low

The most common qualitative complaint. Many members felt 100 points wasn't worth the redemption effort, especially long-term subscribers. Some described feeling "disappointed" or that it was "predictable."

"100 points when you're regularly spending £80+ on wine doesn't feel balanced"
"Both of us tried — got 100 each. So predictable. It made me want to cancel both subscriptions"

🟡 Redemption Friction

Even when the QR code worked, members found the multi-step process (scratch → scan → log in → enter code) cumbersome. Many wanted auto-applied points or in-app redemption.

"It could be auto-updated to my balance, I almost forgot to redeem"
"Annoying you had to enter a code to redeem. 100 points is very small and is what I assume most were for"

🟡 Points Expiry & Value Confusion

Several members were unclear about points expiry, and bi-monthly subscribers felt the 30-day redemption window was too tight. Others noted the Rewards Store lacked desirable items.

"The points drop off quickly if not used, so it felt a bit pointless"
"I get a bi-monthly box but the points expire after 30 days so I won't use them"

🟡 Environmental Concerns

A smaller but notable segment flagged the physical card as wasteful — extra cardboard, non-recyclable scratch material, and unnecessary production cost when a digital version could achieve the same effect.

"A waste of cardboard. A similar digital version would be better!"
"Nice idea but in this day and age, additional packaging is maybe not the best for the environment"

6. Opportunities & Ideas for Future Promotions

Immediate Improvements for Next Scratch Card Run

  • High Priority Fix the redemption pipeline. QR code failures were the #1 technical complaint. Consider a simpler redemption: link directly to an authenticated page in the app, or auto-credit points to accounts with a fallback manual code entry.
  • High Priority Raise the minimum floor. 100 points felt insulting to loyal, high-spending members. Consider 200–300 as a minimum, or tier the base value by membership level/tenure. The incremental cost is small relative to the goodwill gained.
  • High Priority Improve card visibility. Tape to the inside of the lid, use a brightly coloured envelope, or attach to the tasting notes card. Multiple members suggested these exact solutions.
  • Medium Priority Extend the redemption window. 30 days is too tight for bi-monthly subscribers. Consider 60–90 days, or tie it to the member's next order rather than a fixed calendar date.
  • Medium Priority Add transparency about prize distribution. Several members expressed cynicism that big prizes existed at all. Share anonymised stats ("X members won 5,000+ points!") on social or in follow-up emails to build credibility.
  • Lower Priority Consider a digital companion or hybrid. 206 members requested digital scratch cards. A digital version solves visibility, loss, and environmental concerns, though the physical card's tactile appeal was also valued. A hybrid approach (physical card + email backup) could work well.

7. Overall Assessment & Recommendations

Bottom Line

The Sip & Scratch promotion was meaningfully influential on January retention. While 37% of all respondents said it had no influence (they were getting a box regardless), the promotion directly swayed the subscribers who mattered most — those on the fence. Among at-risk members, it produced a 50.2% positive influence rate, with 93 members explicitly crediting it as their reason for staying.

Combined with strong concept appeal (47.5% preferred it over typical promos) and high redemption (82.5%), this format has earned a repeat run — ideally with the technical and value improvements outlined above.

Strategic Recommendations

  • 1 Run it again — but fix the foundations first. Resolve QR failures, raise the floor to 200+ points, and improve card placement. These three changes alone could lift both satisfaction and influence scores materially.
  • 2 Introduce tangible prizes alongside points. The strongest signal from open-text feedback is that members want to win things — a free bottle, merchandise, a tasting experience. Even a small percentage of tangible prizes would dramatically increase excitement and social sharing.
  • 3 Tier the experience by loyalty. Long-term members repeatedly felt 100 points was dismissive given their spend. Consider higher base prizes or exclusive prize tiers for senior Wine Guru levels. This also reinforces the value of staying subscribed long-term.
  • 4 Test a hybrid physical + digital approach. Keep the physical card for its tactile appeal, but send a digital backup via email for members who lose it, miss it, or have QR issues. This addresses visibility, environmental, and accessibility concerns simultaneously.
  • 5 Refresh the Rewards Store. Multiple members noted the store lacks desirable items, items are out of stock, or they've already claimed everything they want. The scratch card's value is directly tied to what points can buy — if the store is stale, the promotion loses its pull.
  • 6 Explore a multi-month collect mechanic. The "McDonald's Monopoly" idea came up multiple times. A collect-across-boxes game could drive sustained retention over several months rather than a single-box spike — worth prototyping for a quieter period like July–September.
